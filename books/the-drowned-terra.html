The Drowned Terra

A scholar’s account of the Ondris and its living shores after the Great Deluge.

Preface

In the years following the Great Deluge, much attention has been given to drowned keeps, broken roads, and the scattering of kingdoms. Men speak often of what was lost.

Far less is spoken of what emerged.

The Ondris, now vast beyond the memory of living generations, has reshaped not merely the land, but the habits of beast, bird, fish, and field alike. Entire migrations have altered. Forest lines have retreated. Marshes have spread into former low-country. Creatures once uncommon have multiplied in astonishing number, while others have vanished entirely from regions where they were formerly abundant.

Most remarkable among these transformations is this: though called a sea, the Ondris remains freshwater.

On the Waters Themselves

The Ondris lacks the bitterness expected of a true sea. Its waters nourish crops readily when drawn inland, and settlements along its shores suffer little thirst except during winter freeze.

This has allowed lakeside farming to flourish with unusual speed.

Villages that once depended upon distant rivers now sustain themselves directly from the Ondris basin through channels and shallow irrigation cuts. Grains, onions, and other marsh-grown crops thrive particularly well in the softened soils surrounding the newer shores.

Indeed, the Ondris behaves less as a sea than as an immense living reservoir.

On Fish and Water Creatures

The expansion of freshwater habitats has produced extraordinary growth in fish populations.

Long eels now appear in waters where no elder remembers seeing them. Large sturgeon have been reported from deeper reaches. Fishermen speak often of broad-backed fish capable of overturning narrow boats when startled.

Whether these accounts are true, exaggerated, or merely tavern inventions remains uncertain.

What is certain is that bird populations have grown along many shore regions. White marsh cranes now winter far north of their former territories. Black river gulls follow fishing fleets in enormous number. Entire floating reed-nests have been observed drifting miles from shore after heavy storms.

The Ondris has become a place of migration.

On the Beasts of the Shorelands

The spreading wetlands have favored creatures suited to marsh and woodland edge.

Red deer populations have increased near sheltered reed-country, benefiting from softer winters and expanded grazing. Wild boars flourish to such a degree that some villages now organize annual culls to prevent destruction of root fields.

More troubling are reports concerning larger predators.

Wolves have reportedly grown bolder near isolated farmsteads, likely following the increase in deer and boar. There are even scattered accounts of massive scaled creatures basking upon distant mudbanks during the hottest months, though such stories vary wildly between witnesses.

The common folk call many such things fiends. Scholars should remain cautious.

On Farming and Settlement

The freshwater nature of the Ondris has altered settlement patterns dramatically.

Prior to the Deluge, many communities clustered tightly around dependable river routes. Now, broad stretches of shoreline support habitation directly.

Fishing villages multiply rapidly. Small ferry economies emerge wherever stable crossings can be maintained.

The greatest danger is not drought, but flood.

Entire shore hamlets have vanished beneath sudden seasonal swelling when eastern meltwaters surge too greatly into the basin. Farmers have therefore begun constructing raised granaries on stone pilings, a practice once uncommon outside river marshlands.

Notably, livestock raised near Ondris floodplains appear healthier than those farther inland. Sheep produce thicker wool. Cattle grow large and broad, though often temperamental.

Some Alari naturalists suspect the mineral richness of post-Deluge soils to be responsible.

On the Character of the Delmarans

I suspect, though cannot prove, that the Ondris has begun reshaping not merely the land, but the temperament of the people themselves.

The old Delmaran spirit was river-bound. The new Delmaran spirit grows lakeward.

Men who live beside vast waters become patient watchers of weather. They learn fog, distance, silence, and storm-light. They become suspicious of horizons.

The Ondris is too large to feel entirely tame. Perhaps this is why so many stories now gather upon its waters.

Final Observation

Many speak of the Deluge only as catastrophe.

Yet nature itself does not mourn as men do.

The reeds spread. The cranes return. Fish multiply beneath drowned stone.

And where stone halls once stood, now children cast nets from weathered docks beneath the evening light of the Ondris.
