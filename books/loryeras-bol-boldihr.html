The Deep Released

"The dwarves did not merely uncover treasure beneath the mountain. They uncovered longing, memory, grief, and the Deep itself."

— Loryeras of Kel Alari

A historical account penned by Loryeras, royal historian of Kel Alari, some centuries after the Great Deluge.

Preface

I, Loryeras of Kel Alari, set down this account in the reign of those born long after the waters rose, that the children of our houses may know the grief beneath their maps.

Much here is gathered from dwarven fragments, Alari witnesses, inscriptions copied before further ruin, and the sad songs that survived when cities did not. Where the ancient dwarven hand is uncertain, I have given the sense as best as counsel and rhyme allow.

The Door in the Depths

In the golden age of Aurumveldor, word was brought to King Ordrin of Bol Boldihr that a door had been uncovered in the depths beneath his hold. Its discoverer was Thoradar, a delver of great craft and greater conviction.

The door was said to answer the legends of Thinrist Firefist and Thindor Firefist, the Third of Three. Many believed that behind such a door lay not only a tomb, but the grandest treasure ever buried away by dwarves.

Near the door ran a beautiful stream. Its waters glistened faintly with the dust of gold, silver, and certain gems. Thoradar confessed that while uncovering the door he felt he was not alone, and at times believed the stream itself whispered to him from the deep.

The Statue of Thinrist

Before the archway stood a statue of King Thinrist Firefist. Upon it was written a mourning song, ancient even to the counselors of Ordrin.

The night is black, the sky is blotted out, we have left the holds of our fathers,
And Thinrist has returned to the Maker.
The light becomes dark, the night and again night, the day with sorrow tomorrow.
For Thinrist has returned to the Maker.

The song spoke also of the Old Ones, of spirits laboring below, and of a seal made by decree of Thindor Firefist, the Third of Three. Its final letters were worn, yet the fragments warned of false hope, torment, and the mountain weeping for her children.

The First Opening

The counselors urged pause, but Ordrin and his men were eager. The door was not merely locked. Its mechanisms were bound, and molten metal seals had been set as though by hands that meant them never to be broken.

For days they labored and camped beside the door, for the place lay several days' journey beneath the king's palace. At last the seals were sabotaged, the mechanisms freed, and King Ordrin and Thoradar peered into a dusty hallway unlike the cut and measure of the dwarven halls they knew.

The Bronze Door

The passage opened into a vaulted chamber of impossible scale, as if made for giants. There stood a greater bronze door, every span of it covered in carvings.

One image showed a dwarven king striking axe against shield in defiance of a drow warchief, while battle raged around them. Beneath it was a song:

King under Mountain
King of Dwarves
From the depth of stone we call

Heed our song
Fill our hearts
In the name of Thinrist Firefist we call

Speed our hammers
Guide our axes
As from the dusty plains we call

For ahead is the test
Plentiful times are past
In the name of Thinrist Firefist we call.

The key was missing. Again the company lost days to the door, oiling, prying, listening, and arguing, until at last the mechanism yielded.

The City in Stone

Beyond, the walls bore the image of a dwarven king in glory, watching master-workers construct an underground city. The inscription praised the dwarven hope of surviving death through craft.

We carve our flesh into stone
For earth outlasts even death
And when we die, we die alone
And live again in readers' breath.

Here the counselors confirmed that Thindor succeeded Thinrist after his death, though much remained uncertain.

The Songbird Door

At the next door the stream crossed the threshold, and the base of the carving was of the sea. Upon the door a dwarven king stood upon a ship, while a sparrow sang upon a rock among the waves.

The poem spoke first of axe, friend, book, dream, and cherished companion. Yet at its end the song turned possessive: the singer saw the bird and declared that forever his she must be.

Past this door the tunnel narrowed into a chamber that seemed empty until the floor itself was uncovered. There the sparrow was shown trapped in water, while the king looked down upon her from every wall.

Save me from this place
Let me far from here
I can't look at my own face
But soon the end is near.

A stone trap door lay hidden in the floor. It was lifted, and Thoradar led the descent by torchlight, with Ordrin following after.

The Rusted Door

The crawlway ended at a rusted metal door. Its engraving showed a king at feast, and behind him a dwarf with a sparrow upon his shoulder raising a dagger.

The inscription was badly worn, but it named Thinrist, the buried king with the crimson girdle, and whispered of assault with an ignited dagger.

Only after bitter delay did one counselor discover that the key had been hidden in the engraving of the former door. With oil and effort they opened the way into a chamber below, grateful for standing room and clear water.

The Pleasant Pool

In that chamber they camped. The pool was sweet, clean, and delicious, fed from above and drained through the rocks below. Yet the company became lost there for days. They dug this way and that, and their dreams were of treasure and victory.

On the walls were dwarves kneeling in scattered array. On the ceiling flew a skeletal sparrow. The party studied the bird almost to madness, even discovering that it lay beneath the first statue. Yet Ordrin saw at last that the kneeling dwarves pointed away from the bird, toward moss and dirt hiding a tombstone.

They broke the stone away and found the armor of Thinrist where his bones had once rested. An inscription confirmed the body and spoke of the Songbird immortalized by death.

The Sixth Door Bypassed

Behind the tomb a faint wind passed through cracks in the wall. They broke it open and discovered they had circumvented a hidden sixth door and its puzzle. Mossy skeletons lay against that door, as though they had died there in their last moments.

Beyond was a cavern of many rooms. A great battle had been fought there: guard against guard, guard against warrior, warrior against warrior.

The Seventh Door

In the furthest chamber stood an ordinary bronze door. It was faded but readable. Upon it a songbird picked at bones among ruins, and a slot lay in the middle of the Songbird's chest.

Gems, metals and gold, earth's blood and marrow,
ideas, shapes and stories to be told, piercing my mind like finely crafted arrows.
Nothing remains, nothing stays still.
Nobody left here anymore, for life to scare and kill.

Unable to open it, the party searched again and found beneath Thinrist's armor a crevice holding a jagged and unusual blade. It was plainly the key.

They placed it in the slot and turned it. The blade rang like a tuning fork, echoing from its own reverberation. Heavy mechanisms answered, and the ringing built through the tunnels beyond.

The Singer

Beyond the door glimmered a dim and beautiful blue light, flitting along the walls. The ringing became tone, the tone became echoes, and the echoes gathered into one incredible voice.

Beyond a wall sang a lone Singer, who gracefully ended her tune and greeted them. From afar they saw her in the pool, and each was captivated at once by that shy admiration one feels before someone one hopes will soon call him friend, hero, and rescuer.

She told them she had fallen within. She was chained in the deep, and no swimmer could reach her. The author speaks of a construct by which alone she might be freed.

Then the writing laments the terrors of the water beneath her: how the calm surface lied, and the blue light revealed an abyss no heart could bear to see below.

The Deep

The final surviving portion of Loryeras' account laments the ruin of the city, the betrayal of the dwarves upon the valley, and the release of the Deep.

Here the word Deep is not used as a common depth or cavern, but as a name. Whatever Ordrin's company freed, it was not treasure as they had understood treasure. It was a force, a doom, or a living mystery whose release brought sorrow upon Aurumveldor.

Thus ended Bol Boldihr's golden confidence, and thus began the grief by which the land above came to be remembered as Delmara, the Drowned Land.
